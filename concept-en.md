## 1. What is Socratic QA?

### The starting point

Years ago, I wished I could change the people around me. I attended many courses and studied coaching and psychology.

Trying to change a person directly is like forcing a heavy boulder up a slope by brute strength. While you keep pushing, it may move a little — but the moment you let go, it rolls straight back down.

If pushing from the outside is impossible, could I instead build a mechanism by which the boulder starts rolling on its own? When I asked that question, I noticed the one available lever: **the question.**

### Component 1 — Ask into the blind spot

A caveat first: questions that betray an intent to change the other person, or questions that corner them, instinctively trigger self-defense and backfire. That is not the kind of question used here.

When people are told "do this," they unconsciously resist. But when a question is posed, the brain starts searching for an answer regardless of the person's intention. Ask someone out of the blue, "What did you have for dinner last night?" — and before they decide whether to answer, their mind has already scanned the refrigerator and the dinner table. The brain cannot leave a blank, an unresolved question, alone.

So when you ask into a place the person has not yet noticed — their blind spot — they reflexively begin to think.

### Component 2 — Speak the thought aloud

What has been thought must not remain inside the head; the person must put it into their own words, out loud. This matters because people take no ownership of what they are told, but they do take ownership of what they themselves have said.

Being told by an instructor, "Your problem is here," and saying through one's own mouth, prompted by a question, "Maybe my problem is here" — these carry entirely different weight in the brain. Not someone else's correct answer, but one's own words from one's own mouth: only these take on solid reality inside. What is given from outside does not move a person; what comes out of their own mouth does.

### Component 3 — In the act of explaining, discover the gap in one's knowledge

In the process of trying to explain in their own words, the person realizes: "I had only reached this by feel — the foundational knowledge was missing."

It is like confidently recounting the plot of a favorite film, then stumbling mid-story: "Wait — what *was* the culprit's real motive?" That sensation.

In that instant, a small hole opens in the mind — an information gap. And once that hole opens, the person cannot bear to leave it unfilled.

### Component 4 — At the peak of craving, the AI delivers the optimal knowledge

When the answer to a long-held question finally arrives, goosebumps rise, and the knowledge is never forgotten — the "aha experience."

Too early, and the answer meets resistance as mere preaching. Too late, and interest has cooled; it becomes a mere list of information. Timing is everything. And the combination of *judging the timing* and *choosing what is optimal for that particular person* has, until now, been beyond human instructors.

Picture a classroom: one instructor facing dozens of students, teaching the same answers at the same pace. The moment when your curiosity reaches its itching peak and the moment when your neighbor's does are entirely different. Human cognitive resources are limited; discerning each person's peak of craving in a live classroom is, in practice, impossible.

### Component 5 — The person wants to continue on their own (intrinsic motivation)

Look back over the cycle. It was the person who thought, prompted by a question. It was the person who spoke in their own words. It was the person who noticed their own gap and desired the knowledge.

Because of this, no sense of "I was changed by someone else" — and no rebound — ever arises. They spoke for themselves, noticed for themselves, and the knowledge gained from that craving is entirely *theirs*. So they want to continue on their own. This is intrinsic motivation.

The difference can also be described at the level of brain chemistry. When we try to change someone, we typically use external rewards and punishments — "do it and your evaluation improves," "fail and you're scolded." These work briefly, but the effect is short-lived and the brain habituates quickly, demanding ever stronger stimuli and larger rewards, exhausting both sides.

By contrast, the satisfaction of self-discovery is linked to what is often called the "happiness hormone," supporting a durable sense of well-being, deep self-acceptance, and self-esteem — not the anxious "more, more" of external reward, but a calm satisfaction that lasts. This is why the method never hands over answers from outside (extrinsic, dopamine, short-lived) but instead draws motivation up from within through questions (intrinsic, serotonin, enduring).

### The whole picture (the cycle)

The flow above is not a one-shot process but a cycle that turns, deepening awareness a little with each revolution.

### Conclusion

Pose a good question, and the other person thinks around it. In thinking, they notice for themselves — "wait, this is interesting." Small self-corrections begin. Layer question upon question, each shifting the angle slightly, and the possibility opens of profound change.

Do not force a person up the slope. Open a hole with a question, wait for the boulder to start rolling on its own, and at the optimal moment, lay down the path of knowledge before it. This is the core of Socratic QA.

### Scientific grounding

- **Component 3 → Information-gap theory (Loewenstein, 1994):** Curiosity arises from the gap between what one knows and what one wants to know; the gap produces an uncomfortable tension — a craving — that drives exploratory behavior.
- **Component 4 → The neuroscience of curiosity and memory (Gruber et al., 2014):** In a state of high curiosity, the reward system (dopaminergic circuits) and the hippocampus activate together, and information received in that state is strongly consolidated.
- **Component 2 → The generation effect (Slamecka & Graf, 1978):** Self-generated information is remembered distinctly better than information merely read or given (effect size d = 0.40).
- **Component 5 → Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan) and the contrast in brain chemistry:** The condition for intrinsic motivation is autonomy — the sense of having chosen and noticed for oneself. Dopamine from extrinsic reward is short-lived; the well-being associated with intrinsic motivation endures. This underwrites the claim: "because I noticed and said it myself, I want to continue."

## 2. What is original in Socratic QA (and what is not)

### First, an honest acknowledgment

The broad idea — "an AI that guides through questions rather than giving answers directly" — is already in practical use around the world (e.g., Khan Academy's Khanmigo, SchoolAI, research at Dartmouth, standardized-patient systems in medical education). This is not my invention.

### The central originality: AI-driven timing × individualization, aimed at the peak of craving

Socratic QA, by contrast, stands on the idea of *actively designing for timing*: "a hole opens, craving builds — and at its peak, pour in the knowledge optimal for that specific person."

This was never feasible for human instructors. The peak of craving differs from person to person, and so does the knowledge each person needs; in one-to-many teaching, neither could be captured.

### Two further points of originality surrounding it

**One: making the contrast in brain chemistry a design principle.** Extrinsic motivation runs on dopamine (brief, habituating, always demanding "more"); intrinsic motivation is associated with serotonin (durable well-being). This difference is fixed into the method as the *reason* for its practical rule: never give answers from outside; draw out intrinsic motivation through questions.

**Two: where the goal is placed.** Nearly all AI tutors aim at knowledge acquisition and academic performance. The goal of Socratic QA is not knowledge acquisition but **self-acceptance and inner transformation.**

### Conclusion: not a world first, but an original integration (a recipe)

None of these elements can be claimed as "done by no one else on earth." However — (1) AI-driven timing design aimed at the peak of craving, (2) the dopamine/serotonin contrast as a design principle, and (3) self-acceptance and transformation as the goal — bundling these three into a single package, explicitly articulated as one system: within the scope of my research, I have found no precedent.

Note also: this methodology is not limited to any particular audience. It is a general-purpose design applicable to anyone seeking learning or change.

### On implementation (a provisional judgment as of this writing)

The crux of Socratic QA lies in continuously and delicately judging, from the subtleties of the user's own words: *Is this the peak of craving? Am I withholding the answer? What is the next question that stays close to this person?*

Having actually used and compared multiple generative AIs, the author, Makoto Ikeda, judges (as of June 2026) that this delicate design of questions and knowledge-timing is best realized with Claude.

This is, however, a provisional judgment as of this writing; other AIs may well catch up in the future.

## 3. Timeline of conception (when, and what was realized)

1. **(Starting point)** Once wished to change the people around him; attended multiple courses and investigated, concluding that directly changing another person is fundamentally impossible.
2. **(Insight)** Noticed the possibility of the *question*: that people inevitably think when questioned, and take ownership of what they say in their own words.
3. **(Deepening)** Found that in the act of thinking, a hole opens in one's knowledge; that knowledge delivered at the moment craving arises is reliably consolidated; and that this connects to intrinsic motivation (extrinsic vs. intrinsic).
4. **(Integration)** Articulated this sequence as "Socratic QA." Confirmed that it connects into one line with his own role as a question-centered course designer — a *question designer*.
5. **(Core of originality)** Realized that timing × individualization — delivering optimal knowledge to each person at their peak of craving — is beyond human instructors and becomes achievable for the first time with AI.
